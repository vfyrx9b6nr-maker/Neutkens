Finitism and the origin

17 May 2026

In my essay The Cathedral of Cause and Experience and the first blog post, the magic of the origin played a central role. But what exactly do I mean by that? And what does it have to do with finitism?

The reasoning is simple, but the conclusion is inescapable:

If the history of reality is finite, then there was nothing that preceded the first moment. The first moment must have emerged from the void, but that is illogical. The void does not exist and therefore cannot cause anything.

If the history of reality is infinite, then the series of moments that have led to the present is infinite. The series is completed in the present, but that is illogical because an infinite series cannot be completed.

If the history of reality is not finite, then it is infinite, and vice versa. It is therefore illogical that the history is both not finite and not infinite. All possibilities are thereby exhausted.

Conclusion: The origin of reality is illogical. But because it is certain that reality exists, the origin is both logical and illogical. That is the magic of the origin.

Although the origin of something usually determines its properties, the magic of the origin does not simply apply to the whole of reality. I do not mean, therefore, that the whole of reality is magical, but only that the origin of reality is magical. This magic is localized to the origin in the case of a minimalist beginning. What emerges from the void is not just any 'horse' or 'baseball player,' but something minimal like a single point.

On the basis of logic we cannot know whether history is finite, but we can look at the reality around us and form a picture. Finding a simple rule that explains all observations makes it highly plausible that history is finite and that in general no completed infinities exist. This is finitism. However, finding ever deeper layers in reality gives the opposite impression.
