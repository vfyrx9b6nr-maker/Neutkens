Further Reading & Sources

You don't build a cathedral alone. This is a collection of the foundations, the scaffolding, and the vistas that have contributed to the thinking on this site. It is a living list, which will grow as the construction progresses.

The Two Pillars

The Wolfram Physics Project

One pillar of the cathedral. Stephen Wolfram and his team work openly here on the mission to reduce all of physical reality to a simple, computational rule. It is the place to dive into the technical details of hypergraphs, the Ruliad, and observer theory. For anyone who, after reading the essay, wants to understand how a little rule can build a universe, this is the workshop.

IIT Wiki

The other pillar. A carefully maintained wiki on Integrated Information Theory, developed by the Center for Sleep and Consciousness at UW–Madison. Here, the mathematics and philosophy of consciousness as 'integrated information' are set out. The link explains what makes a causal structure an experience. Indispensable for grasping the 'why' behind the Phi in the essay.

Going Deeper: Wolfram

A New Kind of Science (NKS)

The book that started it all. Wolfram's 2002 magnum opus is a visual and conceptual manifesto for the idea that simple programs can generate complexity. It is the source of the 'Ruliad' and the inspiration for the computational universe. A timeless book to browse for those who want to see how simple rules lead to complexity.

Some Relativistic and Gravitational Properties of the Wolfram Model — Jonathan Gorard

The article that pulls relativity theory into the computational universe. Gorard shows how the Einstein field equations can emerge from the dynamics of a discrete hypergraph. For the first time, the Wolfram Model becomes not just a beautiful story, but a candidate for a theory of quantum gravity.

Some Quantum Mechanical Properties of the Wolfram Model — Jonathan Gorard

The twin of the relativity article. Here, the wave equation is derived from the same simple rules. It offers a computational account of why the world obeys the laws of quantum mechanics—though consciousness, it must be noted, still plays an explanatory role in the model.

A Short Note on the Double-Slit Experiment and Other Quantum Interference Effects in the Wolfram Model — Jonathan Gorard

This bulletin by Jonathan Gorard sets out how the Wolfram Model can successfully reproduce complex quantum mechanical phenomena, such as the double-slit experiment, using computational systems. Diffraction and interference patterns arise naturally from the structure of multiway systems, where different histories of the universe are simulated simultaneously. The central mechanism rests on path weights and completion procedures that determine how different branches of reality interact, leading to both constructive and destructive interference. By linking this abstract mathematics to the geometry of branchial space, the text offers a concrete foundation for understanding quantum phase and the wave function within a discrete, algorithmic context.

Going Deeper: Tononi

Integrated Information Theory: The Good, the Bad and the Misunderstood — Barrett et al.

IIT is often criticized, and sometimes just as often misunderstood. This article clears away the misunderstandings and explains with razor-sharp precision what IIT does claim and what it doesn't. An essential reality check before diving into the depths.

Integrated Information Theory (IIT) 4.0: Formulating the Properties of Phenomenal Existence in Physical Terms — Albantakis, Tononi et al.

The most complete and current formulation of IIT. This is the core reference: a meticulous mathematical elaboration of how to go from a physical system to the corresponding experience. The five properties of 'phenomenal existence' are built up axiomatically here. The book to the theory.

What caused what? A quantitative account of actual causation using dynamical causal networks. — Albantakis, Tononi et al.

This text presents a formal, quantitative framework for answering the question "what caused what?" within complex dynamical systems, such as neural networks or brain circuits. The authors introduce a method for actual causation based on five core principles from Integrated Information Theory (IIT): realization, composition, information, integration, and exclusion. By employing system interventions and counterfactual analyses, this model not only identifies which elements are responsible for a specific transition, but also quantifies the exact strength of these causal links. A crucial aspect of their approach is that both causes and effects are viewed from an intersectional perspective, in which higher-order interactions among multiple variables are also taken into account. Ultimately, this framework enables scientists to objectively dissect causal structures in both biological and artificial systems and to resolve paradoxes such as overdetermination.
