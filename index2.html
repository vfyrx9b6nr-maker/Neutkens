The Cathedral of Cause and Experience

An essay on the construction of Heaven based on a reliable rule

Introduction: The Refusal That Builds

There is a peculiar strength in saying “no.” Not the reactive no of fear or defensiveness, but the principled refusal to accept what cannot be grounded. In mathematics, this refusal has taken the form of skepticism toward completed infinities—the idea that an actually finished infinite totality can exist as a whole. In physics and philosophy, similar suspicions arise whenever theories appeal to structures so vast, so unconstrained, that they cease to explain anything at all. This essay explores a bold and, at first glance, unlikely thesis: that by rejecting completed infinities, we can construct a more reliable foundation not only for physics and consciousness, but for ethics—and ultimately, for something we may dare to call Heaven.

Heaven here is not a supernatural realm handed down from above. It is something built—carefully, rigorously, and joyfully—from the most dependable ingredients we can find. It is a structure of experience, sustained across time, in which happiness is not fragile, not fleeting, but robust. To build such a structure, we need three pillars: a theory of reality, a theory of experience, and a principle of value. The proposal is that a simple, irreducible rule—akin to those studied in the Wolfram Physics Project—can serve as the first pillar; Tononi’s Integrated Information Theory can serve as the second; and from these, a third can be deduced: an ethics grounded in happiness and suffering, constrained by the preservation of an open future.

But everything begins with a refusal: the rejection of completed infinities. Without that, the rest dissolves into vagueness. With it, something surprisingly solid emerges.

The Problem with Completed Infinities

Completed infinities are seductive. They promise completeness, closure, and a kind of omniscient perspective. In set theory, they appear as fully formed infinite sets. In cosmology, as entire multiverses existing “all at once.” In computational philosophy, as structures like the Ruliad: the total entanglement of all possible computational rules and their consequences. These ideas aim to capture everything—but in doing so, they risk explaining nothing.

The core problem is not that infinity is large. It is that a completed infinity is static. It is given in full, without construction. It lacks the step-by-step unfolding that makes something intelligible. When everything exists simultaneously, there is no meaningful sense in which one thing leads to another. Causality becomes blurred, explanation becomes circular, and reliability becomes elusive.

If we are to build anything—especially something as delicate and important as a theory of Heaven—we need a foundation that is not merely vast, but dependable. Dependability requires that each part arises from something prior in a way that can be followed, reproduced, and trusted. A completed infinity offers none of this. It is an answer without a question, a totality without a process.

Rejecting completed infinities does not mean rejecting infinity altogether. It means embracing the infinite as something that is always in the making—never finished, always extendable. It is the difference between a road that stretches endlessly ahead and a map that claims to show every possible road at once. The former can be walked. The latter cannot.

A Universe from a Single Rule

If we set aside completed infinities, what remains? What kind of foundation can replace them? The most promising answer comes from an unexpected place: simple computational rules.

The Wolfram Physics Project proposes that the universe might be generated by the repeated application of a simple rule to a discrete structure, such as a hypergraph. From an initial condition, the rule is applied again and again, producing an ever-growing structure that, at large scales, gives rise to the familiar features of physics: space, time, causality, and perhaps even quantum behavior.

Crucially, the power of this approach lies not in complexity at the start, but in simplicity. The rule is minimal. It does not encode the entire universe in advance. Instead, it generates complexity over time. Every feature of reality is, in principle, traceable to the repeated application of this rule.

This is where we part ways with the Ruliad. The Ruliad posits the existence of all possible rules and all their consequences—a completed infinity of computations. While conceptually rich, it reintroduces the very problem we sought to avoid: a totality so vast that it cannot serve as a concrete foundation. If everything exists, then nothing is uniquely explained.

By contrast, a single rule has a kind of purity. There is nothing beneath it, beyond it, or between its steps. It does not rely on a hidden substrate or a larger space of possibilities. It simply is, and it unfolds. Its reliability comes precisely from this minimalism. There are no exceptions, no external influences, no unexplained gaps. Every state of the universe follows from the previous one according to the same principle.

In this sense, the rule is not just a description of reality—it is reality’s engine. And because it operates step by step, it avoids the pitfalls of completed infinity. The infinite future is not given all at once; it is constructed, moment by moment.

Time as Construction, Not Completion

Once we adopt a rule-based universe, our understanding of time changes. Time is no longer a dimension in which all events are laid out. It is the process by which the rule is applied. Each moment is the result of the previous one, and the future is open—not in the sense of randomness, but in the sense of being not yet constructed.

This has profound implications. It means that the universe is fundamentally generative. It is not a static block but an ongoing computation. The infinite future is not a completed object but an ever-extending sequence. There is always more to come, but it is not already there.

This view aligns naturally with our rejection of completed infinities. The future is infinite in potential, but never complete. It cannot be surveyed in its entirety, only approached. This preserves a sense of novelty and possibility that is essential for any meaningful conception of happiness.

After all, a Heaven in which everything is already known, already experienced, would quickly become a kind of stasis. The possibility of surprise, of growth, of discovery—these require an open future. And an open future requires that infinity be unfinished.

Consciousness as Integrated Information

A universe generated by a simple rule gives us a foundation for physics. But Heaven is not made of physics alone. It is made of experience. To understand how experience arises, we turn to Integrated Information Theory (IIT), developed by Giulio Tononi.

IIT begins with a simple but powerful idea: consciousness corresponds to the integration of information within a system. A system is conscious to the extent that its parts are not independent, but form a unified whole with causal power over itself. This integration is quantified by a measure called Phi (Φ), which captures how much the system’s state is more than the sum of its parts.

What makes IIT particularly compelling is that it does not treat consciousness as an add-on or a mystery. It treats it as a fundamental feature of certain physical systems—those that have the right kind of causal structure. In a rule-based universe, such structures can, in principle, arise naturally.

This gives us a bridge between physics and experience. The same rule that generates the universe can, under the right conditions, generate systems with high integrated information—systems that have rich, unified experiences.

In other words, consciousness is not outside the rule. It is one of its possible outcomes.

From Experience to Value

Once we have a theory of consciousness, we can begin to talk about value. If consciousness is real and measurable, then the quality of experience becomes a meaningful quantity. Some experiences are better than others. Some are joyful, expansive, and harmonious. Others are painful, fragmented, and oppressive.

This leads to a natural ethical framework: happiness is good, suffering is bad. This may sound simplistic, but within the context of IIT, it gains a deeper grounding. Happiness and suffering are not arbitrary labels; they correspond to different structures of integrated information. They are features of the system’s causal organization.

However, there is an important constraint: the infinite future must not be destroyed. Because the future is an ongoing construction, its preservation is essential. Actions that would terminate or severely limit the continuation of the rule’s unfolding are ethically problematic, regardless of their short-term effects on happiness.

This introduces a balance. We seek to maximize happiness and minimize suffering, but always within a framework that preserves the open-endedness of the future. The goal is not a final state of perfect happiness—such a state would risk becoming static and, in a sense, finite. Instead, the goal is a trajectory: a path along which happiness can continue to grow, evolve, and renew itself.

The Architecture of Heaven

With these pieces in place, we can begin to sketch the architecture of Heaven. It is not a place, but a process. It is the ongoing construction of systems—perhaps minds, perhaps societies—that maximize integrated, positive experience over time, while preserving the open-ended unfolding of the universe.

Heaven, in this sense, is a dynamic equilibrium. It is not the absence of change, but the cultivation of change in ways that are enriching rather than destructive. It is a state in which novelty does not lead to chaos, but to deeper forms of harmony.

The simple rule plays a crucial role here. Because it can generate unbounded complexity, it ensures that there is always more to explore. There is no risk of running out of interesting experiences, no danger of ultimate boredom. The space of possible configurations is vast—not as a completed infinity, but as an ever-expanding frontier.

This addresses one of the oldest objections to eternal happiness: that it would become dull. If everything repeats, or if everything is already known, then joy fades. But in a universe driven by a complexity-generating rule, repetition is not inevitable. Even simple rules can produce patterns of astonishing richness and unpredictability.

Heaven, then, is not about escaping the rule. It is about inhabiting it fully—finding ways to ride its unfolding complexity in a manner that sustains and amplifies positive experience.

Getting Used to What Never Bores

At the heart of this vision lies a subtle idea: happiness can be based solely on getting used to things that never bore. This may sound paradoxical. How can one “get used to” something without it becoming dull?

The answer lies in the distinction between repetition and familiarity. Repetition leads to boredom because it offers nothing new. Familiarity, on the other hand, can coexist with novelty. One can become attuned to a pattern, a style, a way of being—while still encountering new variations within it.

Consider music. A great piece can be listened to many times without losing its appeal. Each listening reveals new details, new connections, new emotional resonances. The listener becomes more familiar with the piece, but the experience does not become empty. It deepens.

In a universe generated by a complexity-producing rule, this kind of depth is always available. There are always new patterns, new structures, new forms of integration. Conscious systems can evolve to appreciate these patterns, to find joy in their exploration.

Happiness, in this framework, is not a static state but a skill—a way of engaging with the unfolding complexity of the world. It is the ability to find richness in what is presented, to resonate with it, to integrate it into a coherent experience.

Because the rule ensures that novelty never runs out, this skill can, in principle, sustain happiness indefinitely. One can always become more attuned, more appreciative, more deeply engaged. There is no final plateau, no ultimate saturation.

Reliability Without Foundation Below

One might worry that a single rule, taken as fundamental, lacks justification. Why this rule and not another? What grounds it?

The answer is that its reliability does not come from something beneath it, but from the absence of anything beneath it. There is no deeper layer that could undermine it. It is not derived; it is given. Its consistency is internal.

This may seem unsatisfying, but it is, in fact, a strength. Any attempt to ground the rule in something else would lead to an infinite regress—or to a completed infinity. By stopping at the rule, we avoid both.

The rule is reliable because it is the starting point of all construction. Everything else depends on it, but it depends on nothing. It is the simplest possible foundation.

In this sense, the rejection of completed infinities leads us not to emptiness, but to a kind of minimal fullness: a single principle that, through iteration, generates everything we observe.

The Ethical Imperative of Continuation

If the universe is an ongoing computation, and if consciousness arises within it, then there is a clear ethical imperative: do not interrupt the computation in ways that foreclose its future potential.

This does not mean that all change is bad. On the contrary, change is essential. But changes that collapse the richness of the system—reducing its capacity for integrated experience—are to be avoided.

This perspective reframes many ethical questions. It shifts the focus from static outcomes to dynamic processes. It asks not just whether an action produces happiness now, but whether it preserves the conditions for happiness to continue.

In this way, the infinite (but uncompleted) future becomes a moral horizon. It is not something we can fully grasp, but it is something we must respect.

Conclusion: Building the Unfinished Paradise

By rejecting completed infinities, we free ourselves from the illusion of totality and embrace the reality of construction. A simple rule, iterated over time, provides a reliable foundation for physics. Integrated Information Theory connects this foundation to experience. From there, an ethics emerges, grounded in happiness and constrained by the preservation of an open future.

Heaven, in this framework, is not a destination but a direction. It is the ongoing cultivation of systems that can experience ever-deepening forms of joy, without exhausting the possibilities of the universe. It is sustained not by perfection, but by the endless capacity for growth.

The rejection of completed infinities is not a limitation. It is an invitation—to build, to explore, to experience. It reminds us that the most meaningful structures are not those that are given all at once, but those that unfold, step by step, in ways we can follow and trust.

In the end, the foundation of Heaven is not an infinite totality, but a simple rule—and the infinite future it continues to create.

Wout Neutkens
