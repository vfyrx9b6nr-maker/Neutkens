Getting used to Heaven

18 May 2026

On habituation, happiness, and the shadow of adaptation

There is a peculiar comfort hidden in the ordinariness of human existence. It is a truth we seldom speak aloud, but which we all know intuitively: human beings can get used to almost anything. This fundamental malleability of our psyche is at once our greatest salvation and our most dangerous trap. It enables us to survive in circumstances that seemed unbearable, to find joy in the banal, and to rise again after loss. Yet within this capacity for adaptation lies a treacherous paradox: the happiness we find at the end of the habituation curve is not always a blessing. Sometimes it is a numbing, a creeping capitulation to circumstances we should never have accepted.

Psychology knows the concept of the 'hedonic treadmill,' a theory which holds that people tend to return to a relatively stable baseline level of happiness after positive or negative events. This treadmill is not a sign of superficiality, but an evolutionarily necessary mechanism. An organism that remains paralyzed by fear after a predator attack, or lingers eternally in ecstasy after a successful hunt, is incapable of responding adequately to new stimuli. Habituation is the psychological equivalent of the immune system: it neutralizes the paralyzing force of extreme emotions so that we can continue to function.

This mechanism reveals itself most dramatically in extreme situations. Viktor Frankl, the Austrian psychiatrist who survived the concentration camps, described how prisoners in Auschwitz gradually became accustomed to conditions that defy all imagination. The stench of burning flesh, the arbitrary executions, the hunger that consumed the body—all of this eventually became part of a horrific normality. Frankl wrote: "An abnormal reaction to an abnormal situation is normal behavior." What he observed was the human mind, confronted with the unimaginable, constructing new coordinates of normality. Even in hell, the human being can build a form of existence, complete with small joys and moments of connection.

But here the dark side of this wondrous capacity begins to reveal itself. For if we can get used to hellish circumstances and even find moments of happiness within them, when does habituation become collaboration? When does our flexibility turn into a form of self-betrayal? Take the woman who stays for years in an emotionally abusive relationship. At first the insults were shocking, the humiliations unbearable. But slowly, creeping in like mist, habituation set in. She learned to read her partner's moods, adjusted her behavior, found comfort in the good moments in between. The happiness she found in the rare moments of tenderness was real, but it served as a numbing agent that prevented her from recognizing the fundamental injustice of her situation.

This pattern repeats itself on countless scales. The employee who gets used to a sixty-hour work week and ignores the accompanying burnout because the rare moments of recognition deliver a feeling of happiness. The activist who, after years of struggle against institutional injustice, gets used to small concessions and symbolic gestures. The citizen living in a surveillance society who was initially shocked by the invasion of privacy, but has now grown accustomed to the cameras and even finds it pleasant, because it makes life so easy. In all these cases, habituation produces a form of happiness that functions as an anesthetic. It is the contentment of the prisoner who has made his cell cozy.

The human being is a wondrously adaptive creature. We can get used to war and peace, to abundance and scarcity, to freedom and oppression, to love and loneliness. This malleability is the ground note of our existence, the silent engine of both our resilience and our capitulations. The happiness we find at the end of the adaptation process is real—the smile of the prisoner who sees a flower blooming among the barbed wire, the satisfaction of the citizen in the surveillance state who enjoys the convenience. But the question that must continue to haunt us is not whether this happiness is real, but whether it is enough. Is this the happiness we were born for?

In an era of ecological crisis, growing inequality, technological control, and political unrest, this question is more urgent than ever. Our collective capacity for adaptation is both our hope and our danger. It enables us to function while the world burns, but it may also prevent us from putting out the fire. The real battle of our time is perhaps not against external enemies, but against our own tendency to nestle comfortably into circumstances unworthy of us. It is the struggle to stay awake in a world that continually lulls us to sleep, to distrust the happiness that comes too easily and costs too much.

The ancient wisdom that the human being can get used to anything is thus both a promise and a warning. It promises us that we can weather almost any storm, that after every night a morning comes, that wounds heal and life goes on. But it also warns us that this healing sometimes leaves a scar that has grown numb, that the going on of life can mean leaving our ideals behind, that the morning that dawns may be a false daybreak lighting up a prison. In the conscious navigation of this tension, in the ongoing distinction between healthy adaptation and betrayal of the soul, lies the task of a lived life. For ultimately it is not only about whether we are happy, but what kind of happiness we have chosen, and at what price.
