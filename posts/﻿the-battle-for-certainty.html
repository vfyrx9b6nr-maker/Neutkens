The battle for certainty

17 May 2026

"What matters is the concept of a probability verging on certainty."

The Essay sketches an imaginary cathedral. Its pillars are of causal logic, its vaults of integrated information. It is a coherent and hopeful construction. But every cathedral is built on a foundation of assumptions, and where do those come from? Given the model, something can perhaps be proven with certainty, but the model itself is at most highly probable.

The temptation of the conclusive answer

The model I have presented proposes that reality is not based on an infinite, completed Ruliad, but on a single active, minimal rule. This claim solves elegant problems. It eliminates the completed infinity that I label a 'specter.' It gives an ontological ground to IIT. It turns heaven into a construction rather than a revelation.

The temptation is then strong to take the next step: from 'this model works as an explanation' to 'this is reality.' Here lies a catch. Our rejection of a completed infinity is a philosophical choice, driven by an aesthetic or existential need for a living, unfinished process. But the universe owes us no aesthetic coherence. Perhaps our consciousness has properties that conceal the truth. The existence of the Ruliad is not necessary, but neither is it impossible.

The limits of certainty

What matters is the concept of a probability that verges on certainty. In science and law, this is the highest attainable. DNA evidence uniquely matching a suspect, the predicted trajectory of an asteroid based on general relativity—these are not absolute certainties, but converging lines of evidence that make doubt unreasonable.

My construction is first and foremost a coherent research project. It is a hypothesis that exhibits maximal internal consistency and offers a hopeful, ethical framework. The exact formulation of the rule is unknown to us. We cannot program it. We cannot calculate whether the causal structure it generates actually corresponds with observations. The statement that the universe 'began with its own minimal input' is, for now, a metaphysical postulate, not an observation.

Therefore I reformulate the firm conclusions of this essay here with a necessary margin of uncertainty. It is not: 'It is impossible for the Ruliad to be the foundation of reality.' It is: 'An active, causal rule is equally a fundamental and fruitful model for the living process we experience.'

The alternative to absolute certainty

The responsible position is a fundamentally open attitude, based on the unknown. For example: nothing can ever come from nothing. Fine, let us then assume that normally nothing can come from something, but what was there before the Big Bang? We don't know, but if we truly don't know, it may just as well have been the void.

One well-documented, inexplicable phenomenon—a causal event that cannot in any way be traced back to the rule—would undermine the whole construction. To date, no such phenomenon has been found, and if that remains so, it is the true basis of the model. It is not that the universe cannot possibly be otherwise. It is that the universe, to the limits of our measurement accuracy, consistently and reliably behaves that way.
