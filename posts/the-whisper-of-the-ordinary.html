The Whisper of the Ordinary

24 May 2026

On habituation, happiness, and the shadow of adaptation

There is a peculiar comfort hidden in the ordinariness of human existence. It is a truth we seldom speak aloud, but which we all know intuitively: human beings can get used to almost anything. This fundamental malleability of our psyche is at once our greatest salvation and our most dangerous trap. It enables us to survive in circumstances that seemed unbearable, to find joy in the banal, and to rise again after loss. Yet within this capacity for adaptation lies a treacherous paradox: the happiness we find at the end of the habituation curve is not always a blessing. Sometimes it is a numbing, a creeping capitulation to circumstances we should never have accepted. The human mind is a master at transforming the exceptional into the ordinary, the shocking into the familiar, and the intolerable into the bearable – but what happens when that which is bearable gradually makes us invisible to ourselves?

Let us begin with the biological and psychological roots of this phenomenon. Psychology knows the concept of the 'hedonic treadmill,' a theory which holds that people tend to return to a relatively stable baseline level of happiness after positive or negative events. This treadmill is not a sign of superficiality, but an evolutionarily necessary mechanism. An organism that remains paralyzed by fear after a predator attack, or lingers eternally in ecstasy after a successful hunt, is incapable of responding adequately to new stimuli. Habituation is the psychological equivalent of the immune system: it neutralizes the paralyzing force of extreme emotions so that we can continue to function. This explains why lottery winners are not significantly happier than before after a period of time, and why victims of serious accidents that cause paralysis often find themselves at a similar level of happiness after a year. The human mind has a built-in thermostat that regulates the temperature of our emotions, a silent force that continually returns us to a kind of emotional equilibrium.

This mechanism reveals itself most dramatically in extreme situations. Viktor Frankl, the Austrian psychiatrist who survived the concentration camps, described how prisoners in Auschwitz gradually became accustomed to conditions that defy all imagination. The stench of burning flesh, the arbitrary executions, the hunger that consumed the body—all of this eventually became part of a horrific normality. Frankl wrote: "An abnormal reaction to an abnormal situation is normal behavior." What he observed was the human mind, confronted with the unimaginable, constructing new coordinates of normality. The prisoners developed a kind of dark pragmatism: you learned how best to ration a piece of bread, when was best to try to sleep, how to avoid the attention of guards. Even something resembling humor emerged, a macabre camaraderie that made the inhumanity bearable. This is the extreme manifestation of our capacity for adaptation: even in hell, the human being can build a form of existence, complete with small joys and moments of connection. Man is not a passive victim of his circumstances; he is an active creator of meaning, even – or perhaps precisely – when circumstances seem meaningless.

But here the dark side of this wondrous capacity begins to reveal itself. For if we can get used to hellish circumstances and even find moments of happiness within them, when does habituation become collaboration? When does our flexibility turn into a form of self-betrayal? Take the woman who stays for years in an emotionally abusive relationship. At first the insults were shocking, the humiliations unbearable. But slowly, creeping in like mist, habituation set in. She learned to read her partner's moods, adjusted her behavior, found comfort in the good moments in between. Over time, she even began to defend the relationship: "It's not always like this," "He doesn't mean it that way," "I'm not easy to live with either." Her capacity for adaptation, which enabled her to survive, became the jailer of her own prison. The happiness she found in the rare moments of tenderness was real, intense even, but it served as a numbing agent that prevented her from recognizing the fundamental injustice of her situation.

This pattern repeats itself on countless scales. The employee who gets used to a sixty-hour work week and ignores the accompanying burnout because the rare moments of recognition deliver a feeling of happiness. The activist who, after years of struggle against institutional injustice, gets used to small concessions and symbolic gestures, trading the revolutionary vision for the comfort of incremental change. The citizen living in a surveillance society who was initially shocked by the invasion of privacy, but has now grown accustomed to the cameras, the data collection, the algorithm steering every aspect of life – and even finds it pleasant, because it makes life so easy. In all these cases, habituation produces a form of happiness that functions as an anesthetic. It is the contentment of the prisoner who has made his cell cozy, the satisfaction of the slave who is proud of the chains he gets to choose himself.

This paradox raises fundamental questions about the nature of happiness itself. Is all happiness equal? Or is there a qualitative distinction between what we could call 'authentic' and 'inauthentic' happiness – between happiness that arises from flourishing and happiness that arises from resignation? The ancient Greeks already made a distinction between 'hedone' (pleasure) and 'eudaimonia' (flourishing, well-being, living a good life). The former is fleeting and can be experienced under almost any circumstance; the latter requires that a life meets certain objective criteria, such as autonomy, meaning, and connection. The problem of habituation is that it enables us to experience hedone in circumstances that preclude eudaimonia. We can enjoy a cigarette while knowing it is killing us; we can enjoy the safety of a totalitarian regime while our freedom is being hollowed out; we can be happy in a relationship that slowly suffocates our soul.

This insight has radical implications for how we assess and shape our lives. It suggests that we must not only ask "Am I happy?" but also "What have I become accustomed to in order to achieve this happiness?" and "At what cost has this happiness been purchased?" It requires ongoing critical self-reflection, a willingness to question our own comfort zones. Breaking habituation is painful because it undermines the foundations of our constructed happiness. It is like opening the door of Plato's cave: the light hurts the eyes, and the shadows on the wall were so familiar, so comforting.

Yet this pain is the price of authenticity, and refusing it has its own, often greater, costs. The individual who becomes accustomed to oppression loses his capacity for resistance; the society that becomes accustomed to injustice loses its moral compass. History provides countless examples of this, from the 'salami tactic' of totalitarian regimes (where freedoms are taken away slice by slice, so thin that no one protests) to the gradual normalization of surveillance, from the creeping acceptance of inequality to the collective resignation in the face of climate change. In all these cases, our capacity for adaptation functions as a mechanism of collective paralysis. We get used to the heat until we are boiled alive.

This is not to say that habituation is intrinsically bad, or that all happiness arising from it is suspect. The art is to distinguish, to learn to feel when adaptation serves as a bridge to recovery and when it functions as a wall that holds us captive. There is such a thing as healthy habituation: the grieving process that enables us to enjoy life again after loss, the resilience that allows us to overcome setbacks, the ability to find joy in simple things despite difficult circumstances. This is not self-betrayal, but a triumph of the human spirit. Frankl found moments of beauty and meaning in Auschwitz – not because he accepted the horror, but because he refused to give up his humanity. This is the crucial distinction: habituation becomes collaboration when it impairs our capacity for critical judgment, when it shifts our norms so that we come to regard injustice as normal, when the happiness we find functions as a justification for circumstances unworthy of us.

So how do we navigate this complex terrain? The answer may lie in cultivating what we could call 'critical habituation' – a conscious, reflective stance toward our own adaptation process. This begins with the recognition that our psyche is an incredibly flexible instrument that can work both for and against us. It requires that we regularly ask the question: "If I were to look at my current situation from my original perspective, would I find this acceptable?" It demands a certain fidelity to our earlier selves, to the values and ideals we held before habituation set in. Additionally, it requires cultivating external perspectives. Others – friends, critics, outsiders – can often see more sharply what we have become accustomed to, precisely because they are not subject to the same adaptation process. Their uncomfortable truths are the antidote to the comfortable happiness of adaptation. Finally, it requires courage. The courage to be unhappy in the service of a greater happiness, to temporarily lose familiar ground, to give up the relationship, job, or lifestyle that offers a numbing happiness at the cost of our soul.

The human being is a wondrously adaptive creature. We can get used to war and peace, to abundance and scarcity, to freedom and oppression, to love and loneliness. This malleability is the ground note of our existence, the silent engine of both our resilience and our capitulations. The happiness we find at the end of the adaptation process is real – the smile of the prisoner who sees a flower blooming among the barbed wire, the satisfaction of the exploited worker who is proud of his work, the contentment of the citizen in the surveillance state who enjoys the convenience. But the question that must continue to haunt us is not whether this happiness is real, but whether it is enough. Is this the happiness we were born for? Or is there another kind of happiness possible, on the other side of habituation, one that does not demand the price of our soul?

In an era of ecological crisis, growing inequality, technological control, and political unrest, this question is more urgent than ever. Our collective capacity for adaptation is both our hope and our danger. It enables us to function while the world burns, but it may also prevent us from putting out the fire. The real battle of our time is perhaps not against external enemies, but against our own tendency to nestle comfortably into circumstances unworthy of us. It is the struggle to stay awake in a world that continually lulls us to sleep, to remain uncomfortable in the face of comfortable evil, to distrust the happiness that comes too easily and costs too much.

The ancient wisdom that the human being can get used to anything is thus both a promise and a warning. It promises us that we can weather almost any storm, that after every night a morning comes, that wounds heal and life goes on. But it also warns us that this healing sometimes leaves a scar that has grown numb, that the going on of life can mean leaving our ideals behind, that the morning that dawns may be a false daybreak lighting up a prison. In the conscious navigation of this tension, in the ongoing distinction between healthy adaptation and betrayal of the soul, lies the task of a lived life. For ultimately it is not only about whether we are happy, but what kind of happiness we have chosen, and at what price.
